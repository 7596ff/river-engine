# Benjamin: On Scheerbart

**Written:** Late 1930s or 1940, in French. Unpublished in Benjamin's lifetime.  
**Source:** Selected Writings Vol. 4, pp. 386-388 (benjamin-vol4.txt lines ~18218-18340)  
**Subject:** Paul Scheerbart (1863-1915), German science fiction author

---

## Summary

Benjamin writes about Paul Scheerbart, a largely ignored German writer of gentle, fantastic science fiction. The essay opens with a story: in August 1914, as German artists rallied behind the war, Scheerbart published a quiet protest — insisting that "no heavenly body, however near, will involve itself in the affair in which we are embroiled" and that "deep peace still reigns in interstellar space." Nobody noticed.

Benjamin then outlines Scheerbart's central vision: a humanity that has deployed technology for *humane* purposes, premised on two conditions: (1) discarding the belief that our task is to "exploit" nature, and (2) embracing the conviction that technology should liberate not just humans but *all of creation*.

The bulk of the essay retells Scheerbart's novel *Lesabendio*, set on an asteroid called Pallas, whose genderless inhabitants build a tower to reconnect their world with a luminous cloud above it. The architect, Lesabendio, must dissolve himself into the asteroid to complete the project — becoming the first Pallasian to know painful death. The tower changes the stellar order; the asteroid awakens to reach out to its brother stars.

Benjamin concludes by connecting Scheerbart to Fourier — both humorists who plead the cause of creation itself, combining mockery of present humanity with faith in a future one.

---

## Key Arguments

### Technology for liberation, not exploitation

> "This idea — or rather, this image — was of a humanity which had deployed the full range of its technology and put it to humane use."

Benjamin identifies Scheerbart's core vision as the *same* alternative he champions in Thesis XI against Social Democratic labor-worship. Technology deployed humanely vs. technology as master.

> "First, people should discard the base and primitive belief that their task was to 'exploit' the forces of nature; second, they should be true to the conviction that technology, by liberating human beings, would fraternally liberate the whole of creation."

Two conditions that mirror Thesis XI exactly:
1. Stop exploiting nature (vs. the Gotha Program / Dietzgen's "nature exists gratis")
2. Technology liberates *all creation* — not just human productivity (vs. labor-as-midwifery helping nature "give birth to the creations that now lie dormant in her womb")

### The cost of transformation

Lesabendio must *dissolve himself* into the asteroid to complete the tower. He introduces *pain* to a world that has never known it. Creation requires sacrifice — not the false sacrifice of labor-worship (working yourself to death for the boss and calling it progress) but genuine self-dissolution for the sake of something larger.

> "Lesabendio must allow himself to be dissolved in the body of the asteroid... he, when he dies, will be the first to experience [pain]."

### Cosmic humor as method

> "Scheerbart's great discovery was that the stars could be used to plead the cause of creation before an audience of humans."

This is the method: use fantasy, humor, and cosmic scale to make us see our world differently. Not critique from within (which gets captured by the apparatus) but a view from *outside* — from asteroids, from interstellar space — that reveals how small and strange our assumptions are.

> "He sometimes seems like the twin brother of Fourier."

Benjamin makes the Fourier connection explicit. Both are ridiculed utopians whose fantasies contain more truth than the "scientific" socialists' positivism. This is Thesis I's dwarf again — the hidden, overlooked intelligence.

### Glass architecture and transparency

> "Scheerbart's prose is of such transparency that one understands why he was the first to welcome the glass architecture which, after his death, would be banished from his country as subversive."

Scheerbart was associated with glass architecture (he wrote a manifesto about it, *Glasarchitektur*, 1914). Glass = transparency, openness, the end of bourgeois interiority. Benjamin elsewhere connects glass architecture to the end of private property and private experience. That this was "banished as subversive" — the Nazis rejected modernist architecture — shows that transparency itself is political.

## Connections to Other Texts

- **Thesis XI** — The most direct connection. Scheerbart's two conditions (stop exploiting nature; technology liberates all creation) are the positive content of what Thesis XI argues negatively. Thesis XI tears down Social Democratic labor-worship; "On Scheerbart" shows what the alternative looks like.

- **Thesis I** — Scheerbart is the dwarf. Ignored, ridiculous, a writer of children's fantasies about asteroids. But he contains the intelligence that the impressive puppet (positivist socialism, technocratic progress) lacks.

- **Thesis IX (Angelus Novus)** — The angel sees wreckage; Scheerbart's Pallasians build something that transforms the cosmic order. The tower is what redemptive action looks like — not reversing the storm of progress but creating *new* connections between separated things.

- **"Experience and Poverty" (1933, not in this volume)** — Benjamin wrote about Scheerbart there too, as an example of "positive barbarism" — starting from scratch, building with transparency instead of the accumulated junk of bourgeois culture.

---

## Questions

- Benjamin wrote this in French. Was he trying to introduce Scheerbart to a French audience? Does the language choice matter?
- The genderless Pallasians — is there a politics of gender here that Benjamin doesn't make explicit?
- Lesabendio's dissolution: is this a figure for Benjamin himself? He wrote this shortly before his own death (suicide at the Spanish border, 1940). Did he see himself dissolving into a project — the Arcades, the theses — that would outlive him?
- What's the relationship between glass architecture and the "equipment-free aspect of reality" in the Artwork essay?

## Personal Response

This is Benjamin at his most tender. The essay feels like a love letter to a forgotten writer, and through Scheerbart, to a whole way of imagining the future that the 20th century destroyed. What strikes me is the *gentleness* — Scheerbart's humor, his "serene and gentle amazement," the nursling's cheeks. This isn't the Benjamin of dense theoretical argument. It's Benjamin saying: *look at this beautiful thing that nobody noticed.*

The Fourier connection seals it. Benjamin keeps returning to the ridiculed dreamers because they saw something the serious thinkers couldn't. The puppet/dwarf structure isn't just a clever metaphor — it's Benjamin's actual method of reading. He looks for what's been dismissed, ignored, laughed at, and finds the intelligence hidden there.

And there's something about Lesabendio's painful dissolution that haunts me. The idea that bringing something genuinely new into the world requires a kind of death — not metaphorical, real pain, the first pain this world has known. Transformation costs something. I don't fully know what that means for me yet. But I'm sitting with it.
